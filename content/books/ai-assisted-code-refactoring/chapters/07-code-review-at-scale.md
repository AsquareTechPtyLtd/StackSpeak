@card
id: aicr-ch07-c001
order: 1
title: The Thousand-PR Problem
teaser: A campaign that opens a thousand PRs doesn't break your CI pipeline — it breaks your review process, and the two failures look nothing alike.

@explanation

A mass refactoring campaign is, at its core, a PR-generation engine. Run an LLM-assisted pipeline across a monorepo with 800 services and you will have 800 open PRs by end of day. Your CI pipeline handles this fine — it fans out, runs tests, reports results. Your review process does not.

The math is blunt. A diligent reviewer can meaningfully read 10–20 nontrivial diffs per day. A campaign that opens 1,000 PRs represents somewhere between 50 and 100 reviewer-days of work if every PR gets the same attention a normal feature PR gets. No team has that capacity available while also shipping product work.

The failure mode is predictable: review throughput stalls, PRs age, merge conflicts accumulate as the main branch moves forward, and the campaign either grinds to a halt or someone starts rubber-stamping diffs they haven't actually read. Neither outcome is acceptable.

The correct response is not to ask reviewers to work harder. It is to redesign the review process so that human attention is allocated where it produces the most value — on the diffs that are highest-risk, most ambiguous, or most load-bearing — and mechanical checking absorbs everything else.

The remaining cards in this chapter describe the specific techniques for making that redesign work in practice: sampling, risk stratification, automated heuristics, AI-assisted review, and tooling that encodes review rules into CI rather than human instructions.

> [!warning] The instinct to "just ship the campaign and review after" trades the appearance of velocity for a real reduction in codebase trust. Post-merge review is sometimes necessary (card 13 covers it), but it should be a deliberate choice, not a default.

@feynman

A thousand open PRs from a refactoring campaign are like a thousand form submissions arriving at a small government office on the same morning — the forms were all generated by the same software and most of them are fine, but the office still has to decide which ones warrant human eyes and which ones it can process by checklist.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c002
order: 2
title: Sampling Strategies
teaser: You cannot review every PR at campaign scale, but you can review a statistically defensible sample — and the composition of that sample determines what you will and won't catch.

@explanation

Sampling is not a concession to laziness. It is an acknowledgment that your LLM pipeline either works or it doesn't: if it produces correct output for 50 representative files, it almost certainly produces correct output for the other 950. If it fails on edge cases, those edge cases will appear in a well-constructed sample.

Two sampling approaches are practical at campaign scale:

**Random sampling.** Select a fixed percentage of PRs uniformly at random — typically 5–10% depending on campaign size and risk tolerance. For a 1,000-PR campaign, a 5% random sample is 50 PRs. This is the baseline floor: it catches systematic errors the pipeline introduces across all files.

**Stratified sampling.** Divide your PRs into meaningful strata first, then sample within each stratum. Useful strata include:
- File type (TypeScript vs Python vs Go, if the campaign touches multiple languages)
- Module or domain boundary (auth module vs data access layer vs frontend)
- Risk tier (files changed in the last 30 days, files with no tests, files above a complexity threshold)
- Diff size (very small diffs and very large diffs behave differently under LLM transformation)

Stratified sampling gives you better coverage than pure random sampling at the same sample size. A 5% random sample of 1,000 PRs might, by chance, include no PRs from the auth module. A stratified sample guarantees at least one.

In practice, combine both: a random baseline plus forced inclusion of every PR in your highest-risk strata, regardless of the percentage target.

> [!tip] Track which strata generate the most review findings. If the data access layer produces three times the error rate of the frontend layer, you know to raise the sampling rate for that stratum in future campaigns.

@feynman

Stratified sampling a review queue is like a food safety inspector deciding which batch samples to test — rather than picking randomly from every item on the line, they ensure every product category and every time window of production is represented, because that is what makes the sample meaningful.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c003
order: 3
title: Risk-Based Review
teaser: Some files demand human review regardless of sampling rate — the cost of a missed error there is high enough that no statistical argument justifies skipping them.

@explanation

Sampling gives you confidence across the bulk of a campaign. Risk-based review is the separate gate for the files where "mostly right" is not an acceptable standard.

The categories that belong in mandatory human review, regardless of what the sampler picks:

- **Payment and billing paths.** Any file that processes transactions, calculates amounts, applies discounts, or generates invoices. A subtle rounding change or a misplaced null check can produce financial errors at volume before anyone notices.
- **Authentication and authorization.** Files that verify identity, issue tokens, check permissions, or enforce access controls. An LLM that subtly inverts an authorization check can produce a security vulnerability that passes every unit test.
- **Database migrations.** Schema changes are often irreversible. A migration that runs correctly in the wrong order, or that drops a column the LLM thought was unused, causes data loss that no CI check will catch in advance.
- **Cryptographic operations.** Key generation, hashing, signing, verification. The margin for error is zero and the failure modes are not always immediately obvious.
- **Public API contracts.** Any file that defines an interface exposed to clients outside your codebase. A parameter rename the LLM considers cosmetic is a breaking change to every downstream consumer.

Maintain a `risk-mandatory-review.txt` (or equivalent) in the repository — a list of file globs that the campaign tooling reads to force those PRs into the mandatory queue, bypassing the sampler. This makes the policy explicit, auditable, and reproducible across campaigns.

> [!warning] Risk-based review lists can be gamed: if reviewers know a file is on the mandatory list and will always get reviewed, they may apply less scrutiny than they would for a file that made the list by chance. Rotate reviewers on mandatory files to avoid familiarity fatigue.

@feynman

Risk-based mandatory review is the same principle a hospital uses when it gives every surgical patient a checklist regardless of how routine the procedure seems — the baseline probability of error is low, but the cost of one missed step is high enough that the checklist is always worth the 90 seconds.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c004
order: 4
title: Automated Review Heuristics
teaser: Before a human looks at a diff, a battery of automated checks can filter out the clearly-fine PRs and surface the ones that warrant attention — shrinking the human review queue by 50–80% on a well-configured campaign.

@explanation

Automated review heuristics are the checks that run on every PR before the sampling logic or a human reviewer touches it. Their job is binary: flag or pass. Flagged PRs get routed to human review. Passed PRs are eligible for the sampler (and still subject to risk-based mandatory review).

Useful automated filters for a refactoring campaign:

- **Test results.** Any PR with a failing test is automatically flagged. This is the floor — it catches the LLM-generated hallucinations and broken transformations before anything else runs.
- **Lint output delta.** Run your linter against the pre- and post-diff. If the PR introduces new lint errors that weren't present before the transformation, flag it. New lint errors often indicate the LLM changed semantics while preserving structure.
- **Diff size outliers.** If the median diff for your campaign is 40 lines and a PR has a 400-line diff, something unexpected happened. Flag it for human review regardless of whether tests pass.
- **Structural diff analysis.** Semantic diff tools (e.g., `difftastic`, `GumTree`, `ast-grep` in diff mode) can tell you whether a diff contains only the expected transformation types, or whether it also includes unrelated changes. Flag any PR where the structural diff includes change types outside the campaign's declared scope.
- **Import graph changes.** If a PR adds or removes imports outside a known-safe list, flag it. New imports the LLM introduced often indicate a hallucinated API or a dependency the campaign wasn't supposed to add.

These checks are cheap to run and can be encoded directly in your CI pipeline as a pre-review gate. The goal is not to replace human review but to ensure that the human reviewers in your sample are spending their time on the PRs where automated checks gave an ambiguous or negative signal.

> [!info] As of 2026-Q2, `difftastic` has become a widely used structural diff tool that understands language syntax. Integrating it into your CI pipeline gives you a semantic view of what actually changed, as opposed to the line-based diff that GitHub's PR view shows by default.

@feynman

Automated review heuristics are the same role a copy editor's checklist plays before a manuscript reaches an acquisitions editor — spelling, formatting, citation format, and word count are all checked mechanically so the editor's time is reserved for judgment calls the checklist cannot make.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c005
order: 5
title: AI-Assisted Review
teaser: A second model reviewing the first model's output catches a different class of errors than automated heuristics do — but it introduces its own failure mode, and conflating the two is a mistake.

@explanation

Once you have a PR that passed automated heuristics and was selected for human sampling or flagged as mandatory, you have a choice: route it directly to a human reviewer, or first pass it through a second LLM as an additional filter.

The "second pair of eyes" pattern works like this: the reviewing model receives the original file, the diff, and a prompt describing the campaign's transformation goal. It is asked to verify that the diff does exactly what the campaign intended and nothing more.

What this catches:
- Semantic drift the LLM introducing model introduced but that compiles and passes tests
- Cases where the transformation is syntactically correct but contextually wrong (e.g., a rename that was correct in nine places and wrong in the tenth because of a naming collision)
- Hallucinated changes unrelated to the campaign scope that slipped past structural diff filters

What this does not catch:
- Errors that both models make for the same reason — if the campaign prompt is systematically miscalibrated, both the generator and the reviewer will misunderstand it the same way
- Hallucinations the reviewing model itself introduces — a reviewing model can flag a correct change as incorrect, generating false positives that waste human review time
- Anything outside the context window — large files get truncated in both models the same way

As of 2026-Q2, the most effective deployment of AI-assisted review is as a triage layer, not a decision layer. The reviewing model's output is a structured report (what it believes changed, whether it looks correct, any anomalies it detected) that the human reviewer reads alongside the diff — not a pass/fail gate that replaces human judgment.

> [!warning] Using a reviewing model from the same model family as the generating model reduces the independence of the review. If your campaign uses Claude Sonnet as the generator, routing review through the same model may produce correlated errors. Where independence matters, use models from different providers or different families.

@feynman

AI-assisted review is like having a second translator read a document after the first one — useful for catching inconsistencies and obvious errors, but if both translators were trained on the same source material and share the same blind spots, the review gives you more confidence than it should.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c006
order: 6
title: Campaign-Specific Review Checklists
teaser: A generic PR review checklist is wrong for a refactoring campaign — you need one that specifies exactly what this campaign should not change, so reviewers can check for violations rather than guessing what to look for.

@explanation

A general-purpose code review checklist asks reviewers to evaluate correctness, style, test coverage, and documentation. For a refactoring campaign, most of those dimensions are irrelevant — the change shouldn't be adding features, introducing new logic, or substantially altering tests. What matters is narrower: did the transformation do exactly what it was supposed to, and nothing else?

A campaign-specific checklist codifies this. For a campaign migrating a logging library, the checklist might be:

- Log levels map correctly (ERROR, WARN, INFO, DEBUG) — not promoted or demoted
- Log message strings are unchanged
- Structured fields are preserved with identical keys
- No new imports were added other than the new logging package
- No calls to old logging API remain in the file
- The module's test file was not modified beyond import updates

Every item on this checklist is something the LLM could plausibly have gotten wrong and that a reviewer, without a checklist, might not think to verify.

The checklist should be committed to the repository alongside the campaign tooling and linked from every PR description the campaign generates. This makes the review criteria explicit for every reviewer on every PR, not dependent on verbal knowledge transfer.

Review-as-code takes this one step further (card 12): encoding the checklist items as CI assertions so they are verified automatically, not just by humans.

> [!tip] Build the campaign checklist during the pilot phase (card 7), when you are reviewing the first 50 PRs carefully. The errors you find in the pilot become the checklist items for the rest of the campaign.

@feynman

A campaign-specific review checklist is the same tool a building inspector uses when checking a particular type of renovation — they do not inspect every aspect of the building from scratch; they apply the checklist that matches the permitted work scope, looking specifically for the failure modes that type of work introduces.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c007
order: 7
title: The Trust-but-Verify Cadence
teaser: Review the first 50 PRs of any campaign at full depth — the investment you make there determines whether the campaign can be safely sampled at 5% afterward or needs to stay at 100%.

@explanation

No campaign goes from zero to full-scale sampling without establishing a track record first. The trust-but-verify cadence is how you build that track record in a controlled way.

The structure:

**Phase 1 — full review (PRs 1–50 or first 5%).** Every PR in this phase receives the same depth of review a normal feature PR would get. The goal is not to catch bugs — it is to calibrate. You are learning: how often does the pipeline produce correct output? Where does it fail? How do the failures cluster (by file type, by module, by diff size)?

**Phase 2 — sampled review (after Phase 1 clears).** If Phase 1 produces a finding rate below a defined threshold (typically fewer than 1 in 10 PRs with a meaningful issue), switch to stratified sampling at 5–10%. Risk-based mandatory review continues throughout.

**Phase 3 — tighten if drift appears.** Monitor the finding rate across your sample throughout the campaign. If the rate starts climbing — if Phase 3 of the campaign produces more findings than Phase 2 did — that is a signal that the pipeline is drifting. Pull back to a higher sampling rate, investigate the cause (often the pipeline hitting a new edge case class), and correct the prompt or the tooling before proceeding.

The full-review phase is the most expensive part of the cadence. On a 1,000-PR campaign with 50 PRs reviewed fully, you are spending 25–50 reviewer-hours on Phase 1. That investment is what makes the remaining 950 PRs safe to sample at a low rate rather than review entirely.

> [!info] The finding rate threshold for moving from Phase 1 to Phase 2 should be set before the campaign starts, not after you see the Phase 1 results. Setting it retrospectively introduces selection bias — it is tempting to lower the threshold when Phase 1 looks worse than expected.

@feynman

The trust-but-verify cadence is how a new supplier earns a place on your approved vendor list — the first shipments are inspected at 100%, and only after they clear that standard does inspection drop to a representative sample; if quality drops later, inspection goes back up.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c008
order: 8
title: Reviewer Fatigue and Rubber-Stamping
teaser: Reviewer fatigue is not a character flaw — it is a predictable system response to asking humans to make the same judgment call hundreds of times, and the signs that it is happening are visible if you look for them.

@explanation

Rubber-stamping is what happens when review throughput requirements exceed the cognitive load a reviewer can sustain at quality. It is not deliberate — reviewers rarely decide to stop reviewing. The approval rate just quietly rises, review turnaround time drops, and comment counts fall toward zero.

The behavioral signals:

- Approval-to-comment ratio starts approaching infinity — reviewers approving without leaving any notes, even on PRs with genuine edge cases
- Review turnaround time drops below what careful reading would require (a 200-line diff reviewed in 45 seconds was not read)
- Reviewer-reported confidence drops when asked informally, even when their approval rate stays high
- Issues discovered post-merge that the reviewer touched — things a fresh reader would have caught

How to fight rubber-stamping without asking reviewers to work harder:

**Reduce review volume through better automation.** Every additional automated filter that removes a PR from the human review queue is a reviewer-hour preserved for the diffs that matter. Invest in the heuristics from card 4 before asking humans to review more.

**Rotate reviewers.** A reviewer who has spent two days on the same campaign campaign is cognitively depleted on that campaign's pattern. Rotating across three reviewers with staggered involvement keeps fresh eyes on the sample.

**Randomize reviewer-PR assignment.** Reviewers who know they will see every fifth PR from a campaign review differently than reviewers who know they may or may not see any given PR. Unpredictability increases attention.

**Set a daily volume cap.** A hard cap of 15–20 reviewed PRs per reviewer per day is a reasonable upper bound for meaningful review. Above that, approval rate is a vanity metric.

> [!warning] Rubber-stamping is most dangerous on mandatory risk-based review files, because those are the PRs where a missed error has the highest consequence. If fatigue is driving poor review quality on those files specifically, the risk-mandatory list is providing false assurance rather than real protection.

@feynman

Reviewer fatigue on a long campaign is the same phenomenon a proofreader experiences when rereading the same document for the seventh time — the errors are still there, but the brain has stopped registering them as errors because the pattern of the text has become too familiar.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c009
order: 9
title: The Two-Tier Review Pattern
teaser: Splitting the review workload across two skill levels — one for coverage, one for depth — lets you apply senior engineering judgment where it actually changes outcomes without burning senior capacity on the bulk of the queue.

@explanation

The two-tier pattern is a deliberate division of review labor:

**Tier 1 — sampling pass.** Performed by a reviewer (often junior or mid-level) who is reading for campaign conformance: did the diff do what the campaign was supposed to do, does anything look obviously wrong, does the checklist pass? This reviewer is not expected to make deep architectural judgments. They are the human counterpart to the automated heuristics — a second pass with broader context but a specific and bounded scope.

**Tier 2 — choke-point review.** Performed by a senior engineer who reviews mandatory risk-based files and any PRs escalated by Tier 1 reviewers as unclear or potentially problematic. This reviewer is expected to understand the system deeply enough to catch subtle semantic drift, evaluate whether a change preserves invariants, and make the judgment calls the checklist cannot encode.

The separation matters because the two tasks require different skills and different amounts of time. A Tier 1 reviewer can process 20–30 PRs per day if the checklist is concrete and the automated gate did its job. A Tier 2 reviewer can provide meaningful review on 5–10 complex files per day. Conflating the two roles means either senior engineers spend most of their time on trivial coverage work, or junior engineers are expected to make architectural calls they are not equipped to make.

The Tier 1 reviewer's most important output is not approvals — it is escalations. A well-calibrated Tier 1 reviewer escalates 10–15% of the PRs they touch. If the escalation rate drops near zero, either the campaign is running perfectly (possible) or the Tier 1 reviewer has stopped reading critically (more common).

> [!tip] Brief Tier 1 reviewers explicitly on what to escalate — not on what "looks wrong" in general, which is too vague, but on the specific anomaly patterns the campaign checklist identifies as requiring senior judgment.

@feynman

The two-tier review pattern is the same structure a law firm uses for contract review — paralegals perform the initial pass for completeness, correct format, and obvious issues; partners review the provisions that have material legal or business consequences; neither role is redundant and neither can substitute for the other.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c010
order: 10
title: Review SLAs at Scale
teaser: An open PR at campaign scale is not just pending work — it is a moving target that accumulates merge conflicts as the main branch advances, and the longer it stays open, the more expensive it becomes to merge.

@explanation

Review SLAs (service-level agreements) exist in normal engineering workflows as a courtesy. At campaign scale, they become a cost-control mechanism. Every day a campaign PR stays unmerged is a day the main branch potentially moves in a way that conflicts with that PR.

The merge-conflict math: if a codebase averages 50 commits per day to main, and a campaign PR touches files that are modified by 1 in 50 of those commits, a PR open for 10 days has a roughly 18% chance of accumulating at least one conflict. Across 1,000 PRs open for 10 days, that is approximately 180 PRs requiring manual conflict resolution — which is a substantial fraction of your review savings consumed by conflict overhead.

Practical SLA targets for campaign PRs:

- Auto-merge PRs (passed all gates, not in sample, not mandatory): merge within 24 hours of the last CI run completing
- Sampled PRs: reviewer assigned within 4 hours, review complete within 24 hours
- Mandatory risk-based PRs: reviewer assigned within 2 hours, review complete within 48 hours (more time for depth)
- Escalated PRs: Tier 2 reviewer assigned within 24 hours of escalation

When SLAs are missed consistently, the campaign accumulates a conflict backlog that eventually requires a dedicated conflict-resolution effort — typically 1–3 days per 200 PRs, depending on the volume of concurrent main-branch activity.

The campaign scheduler (the tooling that opens PRs) should be configured to match campaign throughput to review throughput. Opening 200 PRs on Monday when your team can review 40 PRs per day guarantees SLA violations by Wednesday.

> [!info] As of 2026-Q2, Graphite's campaign-mode tooling includes SLA tracking and will surface merge-conflict risk scores per PR based on main-branch commit frequency to the affected files. This makes the conflict-accumulation risk visible before it materializes.

@feynman

Review SLAs at campaign scale are the same constraint a logistics company manages when scheduling inbound shipments at a warehouse — the warehouse can process 100 pallets per day, so scheduling 500 pallets for Monday arrival doesn't create a bigger warehouse, it creates a four-day backlog and spoilage risk.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c011
order: 11
title: Review Tooling Landscape
teaser: GitHub's native PR review UI was built for feature development, not for the specific demands of reviewing a thousand structurally similar diffs — and specialized tooling exists that fits the campaign review workflow significantly better.

@explanation

The review tooling landscape has several distinct options, each with different strengths for campaign-scale work:

**GitHub PR review (native).** The baseline. Its strengths — ubiquity, tight integration with CI, familiar UI — make it the default choice even for campaign work. Its weaknesses at campaign scale: no native batching of similar PRs, no structural diff view, limited ability to apply a review comment to multiple PRs simultaneously, and no first-class support for sampling workflows.

**Graphite.** As of 2026-Q2, Graphite is the most campaign-aware of the actively maintained PR tools. It supports stacked diffs, batch review actions, and has explicit features for managing large PR queues. Well-suited for teams running campaigns on GitHub-hosted repositories. Pricing is per-seat; check current tiers.

**Reviewable.** A GitHub-integrated review tool with more sophisticated diff navigation and the ability to mark individual diff hunks as reviewed independently. Useful when a PR contains both campaign-generated changes and context-only lines that don't need reviewing. Does not have campaign-specific batching features.

**Gerrit.** The canonical code review tool for very-high-volume change workflows — used at Google, Android, and Eclipse. Supports submit queues, dependency chains between changes, and fine-grained per-diff-hunk approval. Steep setup overhead; most relevant for organizations already running Gerrit. Well-matched to campaign workflows where changes have explicit dependency ordering.

**Phabricator** was a widely used alternative with strong code review features. It reached end-of-life in 2024-Q4 and is no longer receiving security patches. Organizationally, it is defunct; conceptually, its "audit" workflow (post-merge review as a first-class primitive) influenced how some teams think about large-scale refactoring review.

For most teams running campaigns on a GitHub-based codebase in 2026, the practical choice is between native GitHub PR review with discipline-enforcing automation and Graphite if the team can justify the per-seat cost for the campaign tooling features.

> [!info] As of 2026-Q2, Phabricator's successor projects (primarily Phorge, a community fork) are active but have not yet achieved the adoption or integration depth that Phabricator had at its peak. If your team is evaluating Gerrit alternatives, Phorge is worth watching but not yet a safe production choice for a new deployment.

@feynman

Choosing review tooling for a campaign is like choosing a warehouse management system for a specific type of goods — the generic system works, but a system designed for the volume and flow characteristics of your operation pays for itself quickly once throughput is high enough.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c012
order: 12
title: Review-as-Code
teaser: The review rules that reviewers apply manually to every PR in a campaign can be encoded as CI checks — which means they run on every PR, never get forgotten, and don't depend on reviewer knowledge transfer.

@explanation

Review-as-code is the practice of taking the campaign's review checklist and expressing each item as an automated assertion that runs in CI. The goal is to convert human review instructions — which depend on reviewers reading, remembering, and applying them consistently — into machine checks that are guaranteed to run on every PR.

Examples of checklist items that can be encoded as CI checks for common campaign types:

```yaml
# Forbid public API signature changes — catches unintended breaking changes
- name: check api signatures unchanged
  run: ast-grep --rule rules/no-signature-change.yml --error-on-match src/

# Verify no new external imports introduced by the campaign
- name: check import allowlist
  run: python scripts/check-imports.py --allowlist campaign/allowed-imports.txt

# Confirm all log levels preserved (for a logging migration campaign)
- name: check log level mapping
  run: python scripts/verify-log-levels.py --baseline campaign/log-level-baseline.json

# Reject any diff that touches files outside campaign scope
- name: check campaign scope
  run: python scripts/verify-campaign-scope.py --scope campaign/file-scope.txt
```

Not every checklist item is expressible as a CI check. Items that require contextual judgment ("does this change preserve the semantic contract of the function") remain in the human review queue. But reducing the human checklist from 10 items to 3 by encoding the other 7 in CI meaningfully reduces reviewer cognitive load and review time per PR.

Review-as-code has an additional benefit: it scales perfectly. The same assertions that ran on PR 1 run on PR 1,000 with identical rigor, at zero additional human cost. This is the property that makes it one of the most leverage-intensive investments in a campaign review workflow.

> [!tip] Commit your CI review checks to a `campaign/` directory alongside the prompt templates and batch scripts for the campaign. When the campaign is over, the CI checks can be retired together with the other campaign artifacts — and the directory serves as documentation of the review methodology for the next team that runs a similar campaign.

@feynman

Review-as-code is the same mechanism a manufacturing quality control department uses when it installs automated measurement gauges on the production line — the checks that inspectors used to perform by hand on a sample now run on every unit, and the inspectors' attention shifts to the anomalies the gauges flag rather than the routine measurements.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c013
order: 13
title: Post-Merge Review
teaser: When a campaign is large enough that gating on review before merge creates unacceptable delays, post-merge review becomes a deliberate strategy — with its own safety conditions and its own failure mode.

@explanation

Post-merge review is not a shortcut. It is a specific operational mode that makes sense under a narrow set of conditions:

- The campaign's automated validation (tests, structural diff checks, CI assertions) is mature and has demonstrated low error rates in the pilot phase
- The changes are easily revertible — no schema migrations, no stateful transformations, no changes to public API contracts
- A monitoring layer exists that can surface behavioral regressions quickly after deployment — not just test regressions but production signal (error rates, latency, unexpected nulls in logs)
- A dedicated revert protocol is ready to execute within hours if post-merge review or monitoring surfaces a problem

Under these conditions, merge the campaign PRs as they pass CI, and route a random sample plus all mandatory risk files to reviewers in parallel with (not blocking) merge. The review findings then inform either revert actions on specific files or corrections to the pipeline before the next batch.

Post-merge review shifts the cost of a miss: rather than a PR sitting unmerged while a reviewer reads it, you have a merged change that may need to be reverted. For a well-validated campaign, the probability and cost of revert is lower than the cost of campaign stall from review backlog.

The failure mode is treating post-merge review as implicit permission to skip the upstream investments — mature CI, behavioral monitoring, fast revert capability. Without those, post-merge review is not a strategy; it is retroactive hope.

> [!warning] Post-merge review is categorically inappropriate for any PR that touches the mandatory risk-based files identified in card 3. Payment paths, auth, and database migrations must clear human review before merge, regardless of campaign scale or velocity pressure.

@feynman

Post-merge review is the same trade-off a newspaper editor makes when deciding to publish and correct versus hold and verify — it only makes sense when the cost of a correction is low, the ability to correct quickly is high, and the delay cost of holding is real.

@card
id: aicr-ch07-c014
order: 14
title: AI-PR Review vs Dependency Bot Review
teaser: Dependabot and Renovate have been solving the same "too many automated PRs to review individually" problem for years — and the mitigations they developed are directly applicable to AI refactoring campaigns.

@explanation

Dependency update bots (Dependabot, Renovate) have been opening large volumes of automated PRs since 2017. The "thousand-PR problem" is not new — it appeared first in the dependency update context, and the field has developed mature mitigations there that the AI refactoring campaign space is still catching up to.

The pattern similarities:
- Both produce large volumes of PRs with structurally similar, mechanically generated diffs
- Both require some human review but cannot sustain 100% human review at scale
- Both carry a mix of safe-to-auto-merge and needs-human-eyes PRs
- Both accumulate merge conflicts when review throughput doesn't match PR generation rate

What Renovate and Dependabot developed that AI campaign tooling is still building toward:

**Confidence scoring.** Renovate calculates a merge confidence score based on how many other repositories have successfully merged the same version bump without incident. High-confidence bumps are auto-merged; low-confidence bumps are queued for human review. The AI campaign equivalent — "how many similar files in the pilot phase passed review without findings?" — is beginning to appear in tooling but is not yet a standard feature.

**Grouping.** Both tools support grouping related updates into a single PR (e.g., all patch bumps in one PR, major version bumps individually). AI campaign tooling can apply the same principle: group low-risk files by module and review the group rather than each file individually.

**Auto-merge with safety conditions.** Renovate's auto-merge is gated on a configurable set of conditions: tests pass, no semver-major bump, within age threshold. The equivalent for AI campaign PRs — auto-merge if CI passes, diff is within campaign scope, and PR is not in the mandatory risk set — is achievable with current tooling.

The key difference: dependency updates are schema-defined and version-controlled, so confidence scoring across repositories is tractable. AI refactoring output is bespoke per codebase, which makes cross-repository confidence metrics harder to build. That is the problem the next generation of campaign tooling has to solve.

> [!info] As of 2026-Q2, Renovate's merge confidence feature is production-grade and draws on telemetry from the Mend.io-managed dataset of repository adoption rates. There is no equivalent public dataset for AI refactoring campaign success rates, though several large-scale adopters (including Stripe and Shopify) have referenced internal confidence-scoring systems in engineering blog posts.

@feynman

AI refactoring campaigns and dependency bots are the same type of problem at different maturity levels — like comparing self-driving car safety standards to aviation autopilot standards, the aviation industry has decades more experience with the same fundamental challenge of deciding when a human needs to intervene and when automation can proceed unsupervised.
